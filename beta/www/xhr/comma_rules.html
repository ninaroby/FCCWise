Comma Rules

1. Use a comma before a coordinating conjunction

(for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so, sometimes referred to as FANBOYS)

that separates two independent clauses.

Basically, the comma and FANBOYS join together two complete ideas or sentences.

Example: I like to read, and I also enjoy writing.

2. Use a comma after an introductory word, phrase, or clause that comes before a main clause.

Remember, dependent clauses begin with dependent marker words such as when, as, for, because, and others. This helps you remember where to place your comma.

Word: Finally, I was able to relax.
Phrase: Working in groups, the students completed the assignment.
(Dependent) Clause: Since the new system has been implemented, payroll has been processed 25 percent faster than it had been using the prior system.

3. Use commas around words, phrases, and clauses in the middle of a sentence when they aren’t essential to the meaning of the sentence.

Notice, the comma is placed on the inside of the quotation mark.

Word: By “imagination,” then, I mean the free intellectual and sensory play of the mind.
Phrase: Karl Marx, an important nineteenth-century sociologist, believed in his role as a social thinker to change the world.
(Removable) Clause: Senator McGilvery, who is a Democrat from Rhode Island, dealt fully and responsibly with the controversy his new child care bill sparked.

*Remember: Phrases do NOT have both a subject and a verb, whereas clauses DO have both a subject and a verb. If you need more help with understanding clauses vs. phrases, please see handout “Grammar Terms.”

4. Use commas between items in a series.

Words: I made the salad with cucumbers, tomatoes, lettuce, onions, and balsamic vinaigrette.
Phrases: The three reasons for closing were insufficient enrollment, poor instructional materials, and inadequate funds.
Clauses: If the procedure is carefully planned, if that plan is followed with skill, and if the results are carefully analyzed, we might receive the research award.

5. Use a comma to separate two or more coordinate adjectives describing the same noun.

Example: The generous, friendly man was popular in the community.

6. Use commas before and after a quotation within a sentence.

Example: A wise man once said, “Don’t believe everything you read on the Internet.”
Example: “Tomorrow is our anniversary,” she reminded him. “Don’t forget!”

7. Use a comma before an afterthought or a thought that contrasts or differs from the main idea of the sentence.

Afterthought: For Canada, the War of 1812 was vitally important, far more important than it was for Britain.
Contrasting/ Differing Thought: He was late for his appointment, not early.

8. Use commas to set off professional titles, items in dates, and geographical names

.
Professional Titles: Nicole Smith, Ph.D., will be speaking at the banquet tonight.
Items in Dates: Nicole Smith was born on August 4, 1985, in Fresno, California.
Geographical Names: I was born in Fresno, California, on August 18, 1994.
