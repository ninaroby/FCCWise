MLA Paper Format

MLA is the documentation style developed by the Modern Language Association. MLA rules govern the formatting of pages, in-text citations, and the corresponding list of works cited. MLA is used primarily for subjects in the arts and humanities.

  • White, 8-1/2 by 11-inch paper
  • One-inch margins
  • Heading: top left margin: your name, instructor’s name, course number, and date on separate lines
  • Title: Centered, double space after heading
  • Indent first word of each paragraph 5 spaces (or one-half inch) from left margin
  • Indent long quotations (more than 4 lines/40 words or more than 3 lines of poetry) 10 spaces (or one inch) from left margin
  • Double-space throughout the entire document, essay and Works Cited included.
  • Page numbers: Number all pages consecutively in the upper right hand corner, one-half inch from the top. Type your last name before page number.

MLA Standard Format for Works Cited

Works Cited must be alphabetized and utilize a hanging indent as shown in examples.

BOOKS

Basic Format

Lastname, Firstname. Title of Book. City of Publication: Publisher, Year of Publication. Medium of Publication. Example: Ambrose, Angelica. How to do Well in Law School. Fresno: Haitayan Publishing Inc., 2011. Print.

Book with Two or Three Authors

The first given name appears in last name, first name format; subsequent author names appear in first name last name format. Example: Villalba, Tabitha, and Chandra Johnson. The Social Constructs of the Xanudian Subcultures: Fact or fiction? Fresno: Harmony Press, 2012. Print.

Book with More Than Three Authors

If there are more than three authors, you may choose to list only the first author followed by the phrase et al. (Latin for "and others") in place of the subsequent authors' names, or you may list all the authors in the order in which their names appear on the title page.

Example: Self, Eric, Soheil Partoviamin, and Ray Sanchez. The Social Constructs of the Xanudian Subcultures: Fact or fiction? Fresno: Harmony Press, 2012. Print. OR Example: Self, Eric, et al. The Social Constructs of the Xanudian Subcultures: Fact or fiction? Fresno: Harmony Press, 2012. Print.

Work within an Anthology

Lastname, First name. "Title of Essay." Title of Collection. Ed. Editor's Name(s). City of Publication: Publisher, Year. Page range of entry. Medium of Publication. Example: Lee, Phing. “Cowboy and Gypsy.” The Cowboy’s Book of Travel Stories. Ed. Angelica Ambrose. Clovis: Cowboy Publishing UP, 2011. 313-23. Print.

Article in a Reference Book

For entries in encyclopedias, dictionaries, and other reference works, do not include the publisher information. Also, if the reference book is organized alphabetically, as most are, do not list the volume or page number of the article or item.

Example: "Ideology." The American Heritage Dictionary. 3rd ed. 1997. Print. The Fresno City College Writing & Reading Center

PERIODICALS

Basic Format

Author(s). "Title of Article." Title of Periodical Day Month Year: pages. Medium of publication.

*Note: If the article is not printed on consecutive pages, provide the page number the article begins followed by a plus sign (+).

Articles in a Magazine

Ex. for monthly issue: Fay, J. Michael. “Land of the Surfing Hippos.” National Geographic Aug. 2009: 100+. Print. Ex. For weekly issue: Lee, Phing. “How to Analyze the Lion King.” Tutorial Center Chronicles 10 Mar. 2011: 123-4. Print.

Newspaper Articles

Example: Krugman, Andrew. "Fear of Eating." New York Times 21 May 2007 late ed.: A1. Print. Example: Trembacki, Paul. "Brees Hopes to Win Heisman for Team." Purdue Exponent[West Lafayette, IN] 5 Dec. 2000: 20. Print.

*Note: If the newspaper is a less well-known or local publication, include the city name and state in brackets after the title of the newspaper. Abbreviate all months, except May, June, and July. If an edition is listed on the paper, include this after the date.

ONLINE SOURCES

Article from an Online Database

Author’s Last Name, First Name. “Title of Article.” Title of the Periodical or Journal volume.issue (year): page number(s). Name of Database. Web. Date accessed. Example: Langhamer, Claire. “Love and Courtship in Mid-Twentieth-Century England.” Historical Journal 50.1 (2007): 173-96. ProQuest. Web. 27 May 2009.

Entire Website

Editor, author, or compiler name (if available). Name of Site. Version number. Name of institution/organization affiliated with the site (sponsor or publisher), date of resource creation (if available). Medium of publication. Date of access.

Example: The Purdue OWL Family of Sites. The Writing Lab and OWL at Purdue and Purdue U, 2008. Web. 23 Apr. 2008.

A Page on a Web Site

*Note: For an individual page on a Web site, list the author or alias if known, followed by the information covered above for entire Web sites. Remember to use n.p. if no publisher name is available and n.d. if no publishing date is given.

Example: "How to Make Vegetarian Chili." eHow. Demand Media, n.d. Web. 24 Feb. 2009.

MISCELLANOUS SOURCES

Recorded Films or Movies

Title. Dir. Director’s Name. Perf. Name of Performers. Distributer, year released. Medium (film, dvd). Example: Ed Wood. Dir. Tim Burton.Perf. Johnny Depp, Martin Landau, Sarah Jessica Parker, Patricia Arquette. Touchstone, 1994. DVD.

Radio or Television Program

“Title of Episode or Segment.” Name of Show. Host/Narrator/Performers. Name of Network, the local station (if any) and location, the date of broadcast, and the medium (Television or Radio).

Example: “Fresno.” The California Experience. Narr. Tabitha Villalba. PBS. WBGH, Fresno, 12 Feb. 2011. Television.

Personal Interview

Name of person interviewed. Personal Interview/Telephone Interview/Online Interview. Day Month Year of interview. Example: Johnson, Mike. Personal interview. 1 Jan. 2011.
